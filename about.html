From Brain Valley roots to The Notleys.

The Notleys is a working Essex golf club with a practical story: a River Brain valley course, a few names along the way, and a membership built around people who play regularly.

Background

The club has always been shaped by its setting.

The course sits at White Notley, between Witham and Braintree, in the valley of the River Brain. That setting is not just a backdrop; it is part of how the course plays.

The club is now known as The Notleys, but its background includes earlier identities. The course was established in the mid-1990s, originally associated with Brain Valley, and later known as Lords Golf and Country Club, Notley.

What has lasted through those changes is the useful part: an accessible local course where people can start, join, visit, and play often.

The early shape

An 18-hole course built around the valley.

The main course is an 18-hole parkland layout measuring 6,116 yards from the white tees and playing to par 71. It has always been a course for repeat play rather than a one-off showpiece.

The River Brain influences several holes, and the routing changes character as the round moves through the valley and beyond the railway line. There are slopes, ditches, water, and enough variation to make regular golf feel different from one day to the next.

Established 1995 18 holes Par 71 6,116 yards

Names over time

The name changed. The role stayed local.

The club's background is not a clean marketing story. It is a practical local golf story, with different names attached to the same broad idea: accessible golf in the Brain valley.

Brain Valley

The course began with the valley identity at the centre, tied directly to the River Brain landscape.

Lords

It later operated as Lords Golf and Country Club, Notley, a name still found in older course listings.

The Notleys

The current identity is simpler and more grounded in place: The Notleys Golf Club, White Notley.

Today

The club is still defined by people using it regularly, from long-time members to players starting from zero.

Club character

Informal has been part of the appeal.

The Notleys has never needed to feel exclusive to work. Its appeal has been regular golf, easy access, and a club culture that does not require people to arrive already fluent in golf.

That informality has helped new golfers get started here. It is one of the reasons the club matters to players who might not have felt at home elsewhere.

What changes next

Clearer standards without closing the door.

The next stage is not about making the club stiff. It is about clearer introductions, better guidance, and standards that make regular play better for everyone.

Respect for the course, pace of play, and basic etiquette all matter more when a club is open and busy. Accessibility works best when expectations are easy to understand.

Golf around the course

The facilities support the same story.

The facilities are practical rather than showy. They support people before a round, after a round, and while they are learning the game.

Driving range

Covered bays for warm-ups, practice, and lessons.

Practice areas

Short-game and putting spaces for simple, regular work.

Coaching

Support for beginners and players working on specific parts of their game.

Buggy hire

Available when course conditions and daily status allow.

Clubhouse

A base for check-in, food, drink, and time after the round.

The direction

Respect the history. Improve the everyday club.

The Notleys does not need to become something more elitist to improve. Its history points the other way: stay useful, stay accessible, and keep serving golfers who want to play regularly.

The job now is to make that experience clearer and more consistent, so new golfers understand how to start and regular golfers feel the standards moving in the right direction.
